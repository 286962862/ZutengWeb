Project 1

Geospatial Data Processing & Spatial Change Analysis: Shanghai Land Use Evolution I independently designed and executed a remote sensing analysis project to examine urban expansion in Shanghai between 1984 and 2010. Using Landsat 5 imagery and a combination of ERDAS IMAGINE and ArcGIS, I performed radiometric and atmospheric corrections, conducted supervised classification to extract urban, industrial, agricultural, and water bodies, and generated two key land use maps. Through comparative spatial analysis, I quantitatively demonstrated Shanghai's transformation from a monocentric to a polycentric urban structure, directly linking observed changes to policies such as “One City, Nine Towns.”

Project 2

I conducted a quantitative study to analyze the spatial patterns and driving factors of the urban heat island (UHI) effect in Hong Kong. Using Landsat imagery, I performed land surface temperature inversion via ENVI 5.3 and integrated multi-source spatial data including POI density, road density, population density, and NDVI for modeling. I first applied an OLS global regression model, identifying POI density and road density as significantly positively correlated with temperature, while NDVI showed a strong cooling effect. Population density was not significant in the global model. To further investigate spatial heterogeneity, I employed Multiscale Geographically Weighted Regression (MGWR) in ArcGIS Pro. The results revealed that: · Road density acts as a global warming factor (large bandwidth, consistent across Hong Kong). · Population density and NDVI exhibit highly localized effects (small bandwidths), indicating their influence varies spatially. · POI density became statistically insignificant in the MGWR model, suggesting its effect is context-dependent and spatially variable. The study demonstrates my ability to integrate remote sensing temperature retrieval, advanced spatial statistical modeling (MGWR), and multivariate spatial analysis to decipher complex urban thermal environments and their influencing mechanisms at varying scales.
